The Museum of Modern Art's spring programming arrives with unusual coherence this year. Three simultaneous exhibitions — each independent in origin — have converged around a single urgent question: what does abstraction mean when machines can generate infinite variations of it on demand?

## The Shows

**"Signal/Noise"** occupies the fourth floor entirely. Curated by Pauline Yao, it pairs works by Hilma af Klint and Wassily Kandinsky with contemporary artists responding directly to AI image generation. The dialogue is sharper than it sounds on paper.

**"Color Acts"** feels lighter, almost optimistic — a survey of colorist painters from the 1960s through today, anchored by a stunning Agnes Martin loan from the Guggenheim.

**"Drawn From Nothing"** is the most challenging of the three: works on paper by artists who deliberately limit themselves — no references, no studies, just mark-making from a kind of enforced blankness.

## What It Adds Up To

Seen together, the three shows make an implicit argument: that abstraction has always been about intention, not just form. The machine produces forms. The artist produces meaning through choosing, rejecting, insisting.

It's not a triumphalist argument for human exceptionalism. Several works in "Signal/Noise" honestly interrogate whether the distinction still holds. But the act of inquiry itself — of curators and artists taking the question seriously rather than dismissing it — is what makes this programming feel necessary.

MoMA's spring season runs through July 14, 2026.
