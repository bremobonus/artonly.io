Every year the art world generates more names than it's possible to track. These five are worth knowing now — not because they'll necessarily become canonical, but because their work is doing something real.

## Nadia Osei (Lagos / London)

Osei paints domestic interiors that look mundane until you realize the light sources are impossible. She has three shows scheduled for 2026, including a solo at Tate Modern's new project space. Her paintings reward long looking.

## Tomás Feria (Buenos Aires)

Feria's photographs document the night-shift workers of Buenos Aires — not as documentary subjects but as formal compositions that recall Edward Hopper without the Americanness. The loneliness is structural, not sentimental.

## Chen Wei (Shanghai / New York)

Chen works with scale in an unusual direction — very small sculptures that replicate urban infrastructure at 1:500. Seen in isolation, they're beautiful. Seen as a series, they raise questions about what cities are actually for.

## Maya Thornton (Los Angeles)

Thornton makes paintings that look, at first, like digital renderings printed on canvas. They're not. Every pixel-like mark is painted individually. The labor is invisible; the effect is a productive confusion about the hand-made.

## Ariel Mensah (Accra)

Mensah's textile-based sculptures draw on Kente weaving traditions while refusing to be categorized as craft. The gallery system still isn't sure what to do with the work. That's a good sign.

---

Each of these artists is worth following closely. The art world's attention is fickle; these are names to hold onto regardless of where the market goes.
