A24 was founded in 2012 with the goal of releasing films that wouldn't otherwise find distribution. Thirteen years later, it releases about 25 films a year, operates a retail store, and has a brand identity recognizable enough that "it looks like an A24 film" has become both a compliment and a critique.

This is the tension worth examining.

## The A24 Aesthetic

There's a visual grammar that's accumulated around A24 releases: desaturated color palettes, natural light, unhurried pacing, a tendency toward ambiguity over resolution, and a predilection for specific kinds of rural or coastal American settings. None of this was intentional. It emerged from the kinds of films the studio chose to champion.

The problem is that recognizable aesthetics create templates. Filmmakers now submit projects partly calibrated to what they believe A24 wants. The studio that once rescued films from the margins has become a target.

## What's Actually Good

This critique risks ingratitude. A24 has still, this year, released films that major studios wouldn't touch. It has maintained genuine risk tolerance even as it's grown. The newer directors it's championed — Raven Jackson, Titus Kaphar — work in ways that resist easy categorization.

The healthiest version of the A24 story is one where the studio itself is aware of its own orthodoxy and actively works to disrupt it. The evidence suggests it mostly is.

The less healthy version is one where "indie film" becomes a genre rather than a production model — where the form calcifies and the content conforms to it. The studio's next decade will be defined by which version prevails.
