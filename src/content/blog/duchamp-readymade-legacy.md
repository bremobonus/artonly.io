*Fountain* was submitted to the 1917 Society of Independent Artists exhibition and rejected. It was photographed by Alfred Stieglitz and then lost. What we have now are replicas — authorized by Duchamp in the 1960s — and the photograph, and the idea.

The idea is what matters.

## What the Readymade Did

Before Duchamp, the question "is this art?" was answered by reference to craft, training, tradition. The readymade reframed it as a question of context and intention. Place a manufactured object in a gallery, name it, and it becomes art — not because it was transformed, but because the act of selection is itself creative.

This sounds obvious now. That's the measure of how completely the idea has been absorbed.

## What It Still Does

The danger of the readymade was never the specific objects Duchamp chose. It was the permanent instability it introduced into the category of art itself. Once you accept that context determines meaning, you cannot un-accept it. Every museum installation, every curatorial choice, every collector's decision to hang one thing rather than another is implicated.

What's remarkable, 110 years later, is that this still makes people angry. The rage provoked by conceptual art — by NFTs, by contemporary art auctions, by anything that seems to lack traditional craft — is essentially the rage of people who want the question "is this art?" to have a stable answer.

Duchamp made sure it never would. That's the work.
